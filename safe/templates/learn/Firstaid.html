What is First Aid?

First aid is the immediate assistance provided to an injured or ill person before professional medical help arrives. The aim is to:

  • Preserve life
  • Prevent condition worsening
  • Promote recovery
  • Provide reassurance

Core Principles — Check • Call • Care

  1. Check the scene for danger — ensure you and the victim are safe.
  2. Call emergency services immediately (108 / 112) or your local number.
  3. Care for the victim — follow the ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) and other guidance below.
If there is immediate danger (fire, electrical hazard), move to a safe distance before approaching. Only move a person if remaining will put them at greater risk.

Airway • Breathing • Circulation (ABC)

  • Airway: Open the airway using head-tilt / chin-lift (unless spinal injury suspected).
  • Breathing: Look, listen and feel for breathing for up to 10 seconds.
  • Circulation: Stop heavy bleeding, check for pulse (if trained).

Quick responsiveness check

  1. Ask loudly: "Are you okay?"
  2. Gently tap the shoulders.
  3. If no response, shout for help and call emergency services.

Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) — Hands Only

Use hands-only CPR if you are not trained in rescue breaths, or if you're unsure:

  1. Place victim on their back on a firm surface.
  2. Call emergency services (108/112) and ask for an AED if available.
  3. Place the heel of one hand in the center of the chest, place your other hand on top and interlock fingers.
  4. Keep arms straight and push hard & fast — 100 to 120 compressions per minute.
  5. Allow chest to fully recoil between compressions.
  6. Continue until help arrives or the person shows signs of life.
If trained and safe to do so, combine compressions with rescue breaths (30 compressions : 2 breaths) following your certified training.
If an AED (defibrillator) is available, follow the voice prompts immediately after powering on the device.

Severe Bleeding — Control & Care

Rapid and correct action controls bleeding and reduces shock risk.

  1. Apply direct pressure to the wound using a sterile dressing or clean cloth.
  2. If blood soaks through, do not remove — add more layers and continue pressure.
  3. Raise the injured limb above heart level if no fracture suspected.
  4. If bleeding is from an arm or leg and uncontrolled, consider a tourniquet only if trained and as last resort.
  5. Send for medical help immediately.
Why avoid removing embedded objects: They may be tamponading (blocking) the bleeding; cutting around and stabilizing is safer.

Burns — Minor & Major

Minor burns (small, superficial)

  • Cool under running water for 10–20 minutes.
  • Cover with sterile, non-stick dressing.
  • Pain relief (paracetamol) if appropriate for age.

Major burns (deep, large area, face, hands, feet, genitals)

  • Call emergency services immediately.
  • Protect from contamination — cover with clean cloth but do NOT apply creams or ice.
  • Monitor for shock and keep warm.

Fractures, Dislocations & Sprains

Stabilize and limit movement until medical evaluation.

  • Immobilize with splints (cardboard, rolled newspapers) and a sling.
  • Apply cold compress for swelling (not directly on skin).
  • Do not attempt to realign bones or push protruding bones back in.
If a limb is pale, cold, or numb, seek emergency care quickly.

Choking — Adult & Child Techniques

Assess ability to cough/speak. If ineffective coughing — act immediately.

Adult/Child (>1 year)

  1. Stand behind the person; deliver abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre) until object is expelled.
  2. If person becomes unconscious, start CPR and call emergency services.

Infant (<1 year)

  1. 5 back blows with the heel of your hand, then 5 chest thrusts (use two fingers).
  2. Alternate until object is expelled.

Poisoning & Overdose

  • Move victim away from the poison source (if safe).
  • Call your local poison control or emergency number immediately — provide product name, age, weight, time of exposure.
  • Do not induce vomiting unless instructed by experts.
  • If chemical on skin, remove contaminated clothing and rinse thoroughly with water.

Electric Shock

  • Switch off power source before touching the victim.
  • Call emergency services and monitor breathing and pulse.
  • Be aware of secondary injuries from falls.

Stroke & Heart Attack — FAST & Recognition

FAST helps recognize stroke symptoms:

  • Face drooping
  • Arm weakness
  • Speech difficulty
  • Time to call emergency services

For suspected heart attack — call emergency services immediately, keep the person calm and seated, and monitor breathing. Aspirin may be given if advised by a professional.

Shock — Recognize & Manage

Shock can follow severe bleeding, burns, sepsis, or multiple injuries.

  • Signs: pale, cold, clammy skin; weak pulse; rapid breathing; confusion.
  • Lay victim down, raise legs (unless spinal injury suspected), keep warm, and call emergency services.

Assemble a Reliable First Aid Kit

  1. Sterile gauze, adhesive dressings (various sizes)
  2. Adhesive tape, scissors, safety pins
  3. Antiseptic wipes & solution
  4. Disposable gloves (nitrile), face shields
  5. Thermometer, tweezers, CPR mask
  6. Instant cold packs, elastic bandage
  7. Basic oral medications (paracetamol), ORS sachets
  8. Emergency contact card and class roster (for schools)
Check kit monthly; replace used or expired items.

Activities & Drills (Classroom & Home)

  1. CPR practice sessions with adult supervision (use cushions or manikins).
  2. Bandage relay: teams practice correct bandaging under time limits.
  3. Choking drill role-play (without real obstruction) to rehearse instructions and calm response.
  4. First aid kit scavenger hunt: students assemble missing items from a list.

First Aid Quick Guide

  • Check the scene
  • Call emergency services (108 / 112)
  • Open airway — check breathing
  • Stop heavy bleeding (direct pressure)
  • Perform CPR if no breathing
  • Cool burns — do not apply grease
  • Immobilize fractures
  • Keep patient warm and reassured
