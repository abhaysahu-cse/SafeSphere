1. The Science of Landslides

Landslides occur when the slope's "shear strength" is compromised by water or tremors. Understanding Pore Water Pressure is vital: as water fills soil gaps, it pushes soil particles apart, causing the ground to act like a liquid.

Pro-Tip: Watch for "Hummocky" ground. Bumpy, uneven terrain on a hillside often indicates the site of a past landslide, which is highly likely to fail again.

2. Visual Warning Signals

Nature provides cues before the mass movement begins. Immediate evacuation is required if you see:

  • The "Jamming" Effect: Interior doors and windows sticking or refusing to close.
  • Slope Deformation: Fences, utility poles, or trees tilting downhill.
  • Hydrological Changes: Springs or seeps appearing in dry areas, or river water turning suddenly muddy.

3. Survival Physics: Inside & Outside

Inside a Building: Move to the highest floor and stay in the "Leeward" room (the room furthest from the sliding hill). Curl into a ball and protect your head.

Outdoors: Run to the nearest high ground. If debris flow is imminent and you cannot escape, find a sturdy clump of trees or a building and wrap yourself around it to avoid being swept away.

4. Post-Disaster: The Silent Killers

After the slide, the danger shifts to River Damming. If a slide blocks a stream, a temporary lake forms. When this "debris dam" fails, it creates a catastrophic flash flood.

Post-Slide Rule: Stay away from the slide area. A secondary slide is extremely common within 24 hours of the first event.

5. Types of Landslides

Landslides differ based on material, speed, and movement pattern. Recognizing the type helps predict damage and survival chances.

  • Rockfall: Sudden falling of rocks from steep cliffs
  • Debris Flow: Fast-moving mix of water, soil, and rocks (most deadly)
  • Earth Slide: Slow to moderate movement of soil mass
  • Creep: Very slow downhill soil movement over years
Debris flows can travel at speeds greater than 40 km/h — faster than vehicles on mountain roads.

6. Rainfall Thresholds & Early Warning Science

Landslides are often triggered when rainfall exceeds soil absorption capacity. Disaster agencies monitor rainfall intensity-duration (I–D) thresholds.

  • Short intense rain → shallow landslides
  • Long continuous rain → deep slope failure
  • Saturated soil loses internal friction
If rainfall exceeds 100 mm in 24 hours in hilly terrain, evacuation readiness is essential.

7. Human-Induced Landslide Risk

Many landslides are accelerated or triggered by human interference with natural slopes.

  • Unplanned hill cutting for roads and buildings
  • Poor drainage systems
  • Deforestation and mining
  • Overloading slopes with construction
Nearly 60% of landslides in India are linked to anthropogenic (human) activities.

8. Evacuation Planning & Safe Route Mapping

Evacuation is most effective when routes are pre-identified before disaster strikes.

  • Move perpendicular to slope direction
  • Avoid river valleys and drainage lines
  • Mark routes leading to ridge tops
  • Practice evacuation drills annually
Never evacuate downhill during active rainfall.

9. Landslide Emergency Go-Bag

A specialized go-bag improves survival during rapid evacuation.

  • Whistle (sound travels better than voice)
  • Dust mask / cloth (for debris inhalation)
  • Power bank & torch
  • Basic medicines & water purification tablets
  • Local map with evacuation points marked
Keep the go-bag under 6 kg to ensure fast mobility.

10. Community-Level Landslide Risk Reduction

Landslide safety is strongest when communities work collectively.

  • Community rainfall monitoring
  • Shared siren / alarm systems
  • Slope vegetation programs
  • School & village evacuation drills
Community reporting of cracks and seepage saves lives before authorities arrive.
