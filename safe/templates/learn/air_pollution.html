What is AQI, and why it matters

AQI (Air Quality Index) converts measurements of key pollutants into a single scale that tells how clean or polluted the air is — and what health actions to take. Main pollutants: PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, SO₂, CO, O₃.

PM2.5: particles ≤2.5 micrometres — reach deep into lungs and bloodstream. Linked to heart and lung disease. PM10: larger particles, cause irritation and airway problems. NO₂ & O₃: respiratory irritants; increase asthma attacks.

Sources include vehicle emissions, industry, crop-residue burning, dust, and household combustion. Urban hotspots and seasonal events (crop burning, fireworks) can cause acute spikes.

Preparedness — what to do before poor air events

Principle: reduce exposure by reducing time outdoors, increasing indoor protection (sealing and filtration), and protecting vulnerable groups.
Household
  • Prepare a 48-72 hour kit with sealed bottled water and canned food.
  • Stock N95/KN95 masks sized for each family member; include extra for visitors.
  • Seal doors/windows: tape & plastic sheeting; know how to set HVAC to recirculate.
  • Identify a 'clean room' inside the home — minimal windows, inner room or basement if available.
School / Teachers
  • Create a shelter plan: classrooms that can be sealed, roll-call sheets and evacuation routes if needed.
  • Buy a classroom HEPA air purifier (sized for room volume) or plan for portable units.
  • Prepare child-sized masks and teach correct fit/use in drills.
  • Plan activities that keep children calm and indoors (quiet reading, worksheets).
Community / Workplace
  • Identify communal clean air shelters (community halls with purifiers).
  • Coordinate distribution of masks to vulnerable households.
  • Issue guidance to outdoor workers: stop or limit exposure, rotate shifts, provide PPE.
Note on masks: N95/KN95 are recommended for particle protection; surgical / cloth masks reduce droplet spread but offer limited PM2.5 filtration. Ensure a good seal across nose and cheeks.

During an AQI emergency — immediate protective actions

If AQI reaches Very Poor or Severe, limit all outdoor activities. Protect children, elderly and those with respiratory/cardiac conditions first.
  1. Go indoors. Close doors/windows. Turn off kitchen exhausts and any ventilation bringing outside air in.
  2. Seal the room. Use tape & plastic to cover gaps. Move people into a single 'clean room' with fewer windows.
  3. Use filtration. Turn on HEPA purifiers (or portable AC with HEPA filter). Set to highest airflow. Clean/replace filters per manufacturer.
  4. Protect breathing. Use N95/KN95 masks if leaving indoor space or interacting with others.
  5. Hydrate & monitor. Use sealed water; watch for symptoms (coughing, difficulty breathing). Seek medical help if severe.
  6. Food safety. Avoid eating food left uncovered outdoors and do not consume produce that was exposed during heavy contamination until tested/cleared.
If someone develops severe breathing difficulty, chest pain, fainting or other emergency signs — call the ambulance immediately and inform medical staff about air pollution exposure.

After the event — clean-up, recovery & health checks

  • Ventilate the home gradually when AQI improves — open windows only when outside air is cleaner (monitor AQI first).
  • Wet mop floors and wipe surfaces with damp cloths to capture deposited particles. Avoid dry-sweeping which resuspends dust.
  • Wash bedding and clothing of those who were outdoors in hot wash cycles where possible.
  • If symptomatic, seek medical follow-up for persistent cough, wheeze or unusual symptoms.
  • Document any health incidents or infrastructure damage for community reporting and compensation/assistance requests.
Long-term measures include planting green buffers, reducing domestic burning, and supporting community monitoring networks.
