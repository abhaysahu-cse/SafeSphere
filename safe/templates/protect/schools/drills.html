Why drills matter

A short, calm, practised response saves lives and reduces panic. Drills give children the memory of what to do, teachers the confidence to lead, and communities the coordination to act quickly.

Core goal: make safe behaviour automatic — every teacher and student should know two evacuation routes and the assembly point.

Who does what — quick roles

  • Headteacher / Principal: lead planning, liaison with local authorities, approve drill calendar.
  • Teachers: lead classes during the drill, perform roll-call and supervise students at assembly point.
  • School Safety Team: check exits, first-aid kit, radios/phones, and support quick evacuation of students with disabilities.
  • Students: practice Drop-Cover-Hold (earthquake) or calm evacuation (fire / flood), follow teacher instructions.
  • Volunteers / Parents: support assembly point management and safe reunification when needed.

What to practise (session checklist)

  1. Alarm activation & clear announcement (who sounds alarm, how, and by which alternative channel).
  2. Classroom immediate actions (e.g., STOP, sit/cover/stand — depending on hazard) and teacher instructions.
  3. Orderly exit via primary route; teacher holds the class list and leads quietly.
  4. Assembly point arrival → roll call → swift evacuation of absentee list to administration.
  5. Special needs assistance (student list, buddy system) and safe re-entry decision by headteacher only.
  6. Check emergency kit, first-aid response drill and basic fire extinguisher demo (teacher-led).
  7. Short group debrief & psychosocial check-in at assembly point (5–10 minutes).

Role-play scripts (ready to use)

Run 10–15 minute role-plays tailored to age group. Below are short scripts teachers can use.

Fire drill — primary school (script)

  1. Teacher: "Fire drill — everyone stand up and line by the door quietly".
  2. Teacher leads class out, checks corridor, closes the door, counts students at assembly point.
  3. School Safety Team: simulates smoke sign at location X, confirms evacuation time and notes bottlenecks.
  4. Debrief: what worked, any blocked door, student who needed help, time taken.

Earthquake drill — secondary (script)

  1. Teacher: shout "Drop! Cover! Hold!" and everyone drops under desk (30s).
  2. When shaking stops (simulate), teacher orders quiet exit and uses nearest safe route to assembly point.
  3. Safety Team checks for injuries and flags building damage for inspection.

You can create simple props (cardboard blocks = blocked exit) to rehearse alternate routes.

Child-friendly debrief & psychosocial check

After every drill and especially after real incidents, run brief child-appropriate activities to reduce fear:

  • Simple breathing exercise (count 1–5 inhale, 1–5 exhale) — 2 minutes.
  • Circle time: each student names one thing they did well during the drill.
  • Art activity: draw a safe place or safe adult (helps ES, expressive processing).
  • Referral: teachers note children who are unusually anxious and inform the school counsellor or parent.

Monitoring & improvement

Maintain a simple Drill Log (date, hazard, duration, issues, actions): this helps track readiness and reduce repeat problems.

  • Record evacuation time and bottlenecks.
  • List repairs or changes needed (blocked exit, signage, mobility access).
  • Plan follow-up drills to practice the problem areas.
