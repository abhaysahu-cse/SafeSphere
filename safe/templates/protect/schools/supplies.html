📦 Storing Supplies & First Aid — Schools

Practical, low-cost methods to store dry food, medicines and emergency kits so they remain usable during sheltering and in recovery. Includes inventory tools, printable poster and step-by-step checklist.

Why good storage matters

Proper storage reduces spoilage, prevents medication loss, ensures safe water and reduces disease risk in shelters. A small, well-managed stock of dry food and kits keeps schools running as safe shelters for the community.

High-priority items to keep on hand

  • Dry food — rice, wheat, millets, ready-to-eat rations (MREs), baby food. Keep sealed and on raised shelves.
  • Drinking water — stored in clean, covered containers or water bladders (rotate every 6 months).
  • Medicines & first-aid — basic emergency kit, ORS, antibiotic ointment, wound dressings; store in a dry, cool locked cabinet with labelled expiry dates.
  • Sanitation supplies — soap, bleach, water purification tablets, menstrual hygiene kits, portable toilets or arrangements.
  • Tools & supplies — torches, spare batteries, power banks, blankets, tarpaulins, ropes, repair kit.

Storage best-practices (short)

  1. Use a clean, dry room with a lockable cabinet for medicines and a separate raised shelf for food (at least 30 cm above floor).
  2. Label everything: content, opening date, expiry date, and "Use-by" with big tags.
  3. First In → First Out (FIFO): always use older items first. Mark new stock on arrival.
  4. Keep medicines locked, accessible to designated trained person only. Record dispensing in a simple logbook.
  5. Protect from pests: sealed plastic containers, pallet stacking, insect traps; check monthly.
  6. Keep an inventory with quantities and expiries. Replace soon-to-expire stock by local procurement or requests to KVK/authorities.
  7. Hygiene: wash hands before handling food/medicine and keep storage room clean. Segregate food & non-food items.

Detailed guidance — by topic

Dry Food

Store in sealed, food-grade containers. Keep original packaging if possible and place containers on pallets. Label batch and date received. Rotate every 6–12 months depending on food type.

Medicines & First-aid

Store in a cool, dry, locked cabinet. Keep a paper logbook of access and dispensing. Check expiry dates monthly and use soon-to-expire items first. Teach one or two staff on basic first-aid and medicine handling.

Water & WASH

Store water in clean, covered containers; use water purification tablets or filtration for replenishment. Separate drinking / cleaning water. Prepare a sanitation area and waste-disposal plan for shelters to avoid disease spread.

Pest & Moisture Control

Elevate stocks on pallets, inspect for leaks, use desiccant packs in sealed containers where appropriate, maintain cleanliness, and rotate stock to minimize infestation.

Security & Access

Lock medicine cabinets and restrict access to trained staff. Keep a printed contact list for suppliers and district authorities near the storage area.

Quick checks & training

  • Monthly check: quantity, integrity of packaging, and expiry. Record in logbook.
  • Drill: Practice distributing a sample kit in 10 minutes to a small class group.
  • Assign two staff for inventory & first-aid; keep a backup copy of records offsite or in cloud if possible.
  • Label shelf zones (A,B,C) and keep a printed shelf map near the storage room.

Shelter Storage Checklist — quick

Use this checklist while preparing your storage area for a shelter.

  1. Designate storage room and lockable medicine cabinet.
  2. Elevate all food & water containers at least 30 cm off the floor.
  3. Label shelves and boxes with content, date received and expiry.
  4. Check tents/tarpaulins and emergency blankets for dryness and pack them separately.
  5. Place a signboard with contact numbers and the stock master near the storage door.
  6. Ensure a clean separate area for cooking and waste to avoid contamination.
  7. Assign staff responsible for monthly checks and stock rotation.
