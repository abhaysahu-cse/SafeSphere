👩‍🏫 Child Protection & Psychosocial Support

Simple, practical steps teachers and schools can use to keep children safe, calm, and supported during and after disasters. Includes PFA guidance, activities, referrals, and checklists.

Why child protection & psychosocial support matters

Children react differently to emergencies: some withdraw, others act out. Rapid, compassionate adult support reduces long-term harm. This module gives teachers step-by-step actions (PFA), classroom activities to calm and reconnect children, and safeguards for disclosures and referrals.

  • Safety first: keep children physically safe and account for all pupils.
  • Stability & routine: restore simple daily routine as soon as possible.
  • Listen & support: create safe spaces for children to share.
  • Protect & refer: act on any child protection concerns immediately and follow protocols.

Psychological First Aid (PFA) — simple steps for teachers

  1. Look (for safety): remove child from immediate danger, ensure basic needs (water, warmth).
  2. Listen: be calm, quiet, offer a seat, use short sentences. Let the child lead; do not force details.
  3. Protect (stabilize): reassure: "You are safe now. I'm here with you." Attend to visible injuries or need for medical attention.
  4. Link (connect): connect to caregiver or known adult; if alone, stay with the child and explain what will happen next.
  5. Refer & record: if serious harm / abuse is disclosed, follow safeguarding protocol immediately and record facts (who, what, when, where).
Important: do not promise secrecy if a child discloses abuse — explain you will share with the person who can help keep them safe.

Age-appropriate calming & psychosocial activities (practical)

1. Calming breath (3–5 minutes) — all ages

  1. Ask children to sit comfortably, close eyes (if comfortable).
  2. Breathe in slowly for 3, hold 1, breathe out for 4 — repeat 5 times.
  3. Finish with a gentle stretch and a smile — helps reduce immediate anxiety.

2. Story circle (10–20 minutes) — ages 5–12

  1. Use a simple familiar story or picture book. Read slowly and ask open questions ("How would you feel?").
  2. Encourage children to draw one thing that makes them feel safe today.

3. Feelings wheel (10 minutes) — ages 8+

  1. Show a wheel of simple feelings (happy, sad, scared, angry, calm).
  2. Ask children to point to how they feel and one small thing that helps them feel better.

4. Role-play safety steps (15 minutes) — adolescents

  1. Practice "What to do if..." scenarios (flood water near home, someone hurt, school fire alarm).
  2. Encourage peer-support language: "Are you okay?" "Let's find the teacher" — reinforces routines.

Teacher tip: run these activities daily for the first week after an incident — consistency matters more than length.

Setting up a child-friendly safe space in schools

  • Choose a quiet, well-ventilated room with good light and soft seating (mats, cushions).
  • Provide separate corners: a calming corner (soft toys, drawing), a caregiver corner, and private referral room (for confidential conversations).
  • Signage: child-friendly signs showing safe space rules (no cameras, respect privacy).
  • Hygiene: handwashing station, water bottles, sanitary materials; a small locker for medicines (locked, authorized access only).

Safeguarding: disclosure, recording & referral (teacher checklist)

  1. If a child discloses harm: stay calm, listen (do not press for details), ensure immediate safety.
  2. Do not question deeply — record the child's words as close as possible to verbatim (who, what, when, where).
  3. Notify the designated safeguarding officer at the school immediately (name & contact below).
  4. If health emergency, call emergency services and keep child safe; if suspected abuse, follow local reporting law.
  5. Record actions taken, dates, times, witnesses. Keep records secure (locked cabinet or encrypted file).

Resources & training

  • Short training: 1-hour staff orientation on PFA + safeguarding roles.
  • Printable: "How to listen to a child" poster for staff rooms.
  • Referral pack: list of local health, social services and NGO contacts — keep both digital and printed copies.

Quick actions (first hour)

  1. Account for all pupils and caregivers.
  2. Set up the safe space and assign staff rota.
  3. Offer water, basic first-aid and rest.
  4. Prioritize children with disabilities or separated from family.
